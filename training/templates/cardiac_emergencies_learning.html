Cardiac Emergencies

Why This Matters

Cardiac emergencies occur when the heart stops functioning properly, cutting off blood flow to vital organs. Without immediate action, this can lead to death within minutes. Recognizing the signs and responding quickly can double or triple survival chances. These skills could help you save a life.

1
Recognizing a Cardiac Emergency
Sudden Collapse

Caused by heat sources like fire, hot objects, steam, or scalding liquids. Common in cooking accidents or from hot water.

No Breathing or Gasping

Caused by exposure to freezing temperatures. Often affects outdoor workers, adventurers, or those in extreme cold conditions.

No Pulse

Caused by exposure to freezing temperatures. Often affects outdoor workers, adventurers, or those in extreme cold conditions.

Clutching the Chest

Caused by exposure to freezing temperatures. Often affects outdoor workers, adventurers, or those in extreme cold conditions.

If you suspect a cardiac emergency, act immediately—every second counts.
2
Immediate Actions to Take
1
Check Responsiveness

Shake the person and shout their name. If no response, proceed immediately.

2
Call Emergency Services

Dial 911 (or your local emergency number) and report the situation. Stay on the line and follow instructions.

If others are present, have someone call while you begin CPR.
3
Start CPR

Begin chest compressions immediately. Push hard and fast in the center of the chest.

4
Use an AED

If available, follow the instructions to deliver a shock. AEDs are designed to be used by anyone, with clear voice prompts.

3
CPR on Adults
Proper Technique
  • Place the person on a firm, flat surface.
  • Position yourself directly over the person's chest.
  • Place the heel of one hand on the center of the chest, between the nipples.
  • Place your other hand on top of the first hand.
  • Keep your arms straight and position your shoulders directly above your hands.
Compression Guidelines
  • Push hard and fast at a rate of 100-120 compressions per minute.
  • Compress the chest at least 2 inches (5 cm) deep.
  • Allow the chest to fully recoil between compressions.
  • Minimize interruptions in compressions.
  • Continue until emergency responders arrive.

4
CPR on Children

The main differences between child and adult CPR are in compression depth, hand placement, and rescue breaths due to the size and fragility of a child's body.

1
Compression Depth

Compress about 2 inches, but no more than that to avoid injury. For infants, compress about 1.5 inches.

2
Hand Placement

Use one or two hands, depending on the child's size—one hand for smaller children and two for larger ones.

Important Note

For infants (under 1 year), use two fingers for compressions and cover both the mouth and nose during rescue breaths. The compression rate remains the same: 100-120 compressions per minute.

How to Use an AED

  • Turn it on and follow the voice prompts.
  • Attach the electrode pads to the person’s bare chest as shown in the instructions.
  • If advised, deliver a shock and immediately resume CPR.

Working with Other Bystanders

If others are present, assign specific tasks:

  • One person should call emergency services.
  • Another should locate and bring an AED.
  • A third can assist with CPR rotation to prevent fatigue.
