Fractures and Sprains

A fracture is a break in the bone, ranging from a small crack to a complete break. It occurs due to excessive force, falls, or direct impacts. A sprain, on the other hand, is the stretching or tearing of ligaments—the tough bands connecting bones at a joint. Sprains happen when a joint is twisted or overstretched, commonly affecting the ankles, wrists, and knees.

Identifying Fractures vs Sprains

Both injuries cause pain, swelling, and bruising, making them difficult to distinguish at first.

  • Fracture Pain: Sharp and worsens with movement or pressure. Often causes deformity (limb looks out of place) and, in severe cases, bone may protrude.
  • Sprain Pain: More of an aching discomfort that worsens with joint use. Swelling may be significant, but the joint retains some function.
  • Fractures: Often come with a grating sensation or inability to move the limb.
  • Sprains: May still allow partial movement despite pain.

If in doubt, seek medical attention.

Transport to the Hospital

If a fracture is suspected, stabilize the limb and seek immediate medical help. If a sprain results in severe pain or the inability to bear weight, medical attention is necessary.

How to Splint a Fracture

Splinting immobilizes the injured limb, reducing pain and preventing further damage.

  • Keep the injured person still. Do not attempt to realign the bone.
  • Find a firm object (padded board, rolled towel) as a splint.
  • Place it alongside the injured limb, ensuring it supports the joints above and below the fracture.
  • Secure with bandages or cloth strips, making sure they are snug but not overly tight.
  • Regularly check for swelling, discoloration, or numbness, which may indicate restricted blood flow.

How to Wrap a Sprain

Wrapping provides support, reduces swelling, and prevents further injury.

  • Position the limb comfortably and slightly elevated.
  • Start wrapping below the injured joint and continue upward in a figure-eight pattern.
  • Maintain firm but gentle pressure—tight enough for support, but not so tight as to cut off circulation.
  • Adjust the wrap if swelling increases or if numbness occurs.

Further Treatment for a Sprain

Most sprains can be managed at home using the R.I.C.E. method:

  • Rest: Avoid putting weight on the joint for at least 48 hours.
  • Ice: Apply an ice pack wrapped in a cloth for 15-20 minutes every 2-3 hours.
  • Compression: Use a bandage to reduce swelling but ensure circulation is not restricted.
  • Elevation: Keep the injured limb above heart level whenever possible.

Over-the-counter pain relievers like ibuprofen can help with discomfort. If symptoms persist for more than a few days, or if the joint remains unstable, seek medical evaluation.
